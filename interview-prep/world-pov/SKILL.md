# World POV — Strategic Vision 2026-2033

## HOW TO USE THIS

This is not a list of things to memorize. It's a set of positions
to hold and defend. In an interview, you're not reciting — you're
having a conversation about where the world is going.

The test: can you say these POVs in your own words, defend them when
pushed, and connect them to the specific company you're talking to?

---

## THE RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

Five-lens convergence model. One lens = interesting. Three agreeing = signal.
Five pointing the same direction = structural shift.

L1 Capital: where smart money is betting (a16z, Sequoia, Bessemer)
L2 Visionaries: frontier thinkers (Altman, Huang, Altman)
L3 Builders: what operators are actually doing (McKinsey, Uber, Airbnb)
L4 Domain: industry specialists (Gartner, Forrester, Scott Brinker)
L5 Research: lab to production (Mollick/Wharton, BCG study, P&G study)

## THE 3 DEPLOYABLE POVs — KNOW THESE COLD

### POV 1 — Intelligence as Utility (5/5 lenses)

"I think about AI the way we used to think about electricity —
the companies that win aren't the ones generating it, they're the
ones that redesign their workflows around it.

The cost per token is collapsing. Jensen Huang says tokens are becoming
a recruiting tool — companies are putting token allocations in comp packages.
Altman says one person plus AI can build what used to require hundreds.
Mollick's BCG research shows AI-assisted consultants complete 25% more
tasks, 25% faster, with 40% higher quality output.

The implication: intelligence is becoming a factor of production you buy
like bandwidth. The companies that win are not those with the most AI,
but those with the best workflows built around abundant intelligence.
The interface layer is dissolving — a16z says 2026 marks the death of
the prompt box for mainstream users. AI becomes invisible scaffolding
woven through every workflow, activated by intent rather than instruction."

### POV 2 — The Real Wave Hits Legacy Industries (5/5 lenses)

"Everyone is focused on Silicon Valley AI. I think the bigger story
is the $50 trillion of value trapped in healthcare, industrial, legal,
and financial workflows that have barely been touched.

a16z's 2025 portfolio was 40% healthcare, 25% infra, 20% vertical
copilots. Capital is leaving pure infrastructure bets and moving toward
revenue-converting applications in regulated verticals. The pattern they
identified: AI quietly replaces back-office systems before flashy
front-end apps. The moat in those markets isn't model quality —
it's proprietary data and the patience to go deep in a regulated vertical.

The companies that win this decade won't be the ones that built the
best models. They'll be the ones that accumulated the proprietary
industrial data and the domain expertise to make AI actually work
in a specific vertical."

### POV 3 — The Individual Gets Superpowered (4/5 lenses)

"The unit of productivity is shifting from the team to the individual
plus AI stack. Altman's claim that a one-person billion-dollar company
is now plausible isn't hype — Mollick's P&G research showed individuals
with AI matched or outperformed teams without it.

Jensen Huang says Nvidia will have 75,000 employees and 7.5 million
AI agents in ten years. That ratio — one human, many agents — is the
org design of the next decade.

This reprices every assumption we have about how organizations are
structured. Less coordination overhead. More individual leverage.
Junior roles compress. Senior judgment premium rises. The question
for every company right now: are you building for the team as the
unit of work, or for the individual-plus-AI as the unit of work?"

## THE SEMANTIC LAYER GAP — YOUR SIGNATURE POV

This is your most differentiated take. Not everyone can say this.

"Nadella says SaaS business logic moves to AI agents. Huang says the
factory wins when intelligence is a commodity. Both are right.

But neither is asking the harder question: who gives the agent the
map of the data that used to be hard-coded in software?

When business logic lived in code, the code knew what the data meant.
'Customer ID 4821 maps to this account tier, this billing cycle, this
set of permissions.' That knowledge was baked in.

When you move business logic to an LLM agent, the agent is stateless.
It doesn't know what your data means, how entities relate, or what
the business rules are. Someone has to give it that semantic context.

That's the unsexy infrastructure gap that nobody has fully solved yet.
Data contracts, semantic layers, knowledge graphs, RAG pipelines —
they're all partial answers. The company that solves this owns the
agentic enterprise.

And this connects directly to what [HubSpot / LiveRamp / Amplitude]
is positioned to do..."

## COMPANY-SPECIFIC APPLICATIONS

**HubSpot:**
"The risk for HubSpot isn't that agents replace the product. It's that
a third-party agent layer abstracts HubSpot away. The defense is owning
the semantic layer — making HubSpot the authoritative source of truth
about a company's revenue data, not just the UI on top of it."

**LiveRamp:**
"Every agent needs a trust layer for data — a way to resolve identities,
enforce permissions, and translate between siloes without exposing raw
PII. That's not a new problem — that's LiveRamp's existing business.
The question is whether LiveRamp moves up the stack to become the agent's
data fabric, or stays a connectivity layer underneath agents built by others."

**Amplitude:**
"The next competitive battle in product analytics isn't about better
charts — it's about who owns the semantic layer that agents query.
Amplitude's Data product — the event taxonomy and metric definitions —
is the seed of that. Make it machine-readable and agent-accessible and
you become the analytics infrastructure of the agentic era."

**Fivetran/Airbyte:**
"Every agent needs a reliable data pipeline. The 200+ integration problem
I've lived — schema drift, rate limits, partial syncs, credential failures
— is the unsexy infrastructure that makes every AI application possible.
Data integration is not a solved problem. It's the foundation everything
else is built on."

## TIMING MAP

**2026-2027:**
Agentic workflows go mainstream in knowledge work. AI interfaces become
invisible. Data becomes the scarce resource (a16z: "2026 is the year
of data constraints, not compute constraints"). First $0→$1B AI-native
companies emerge. Legacy vertical penetration begins.

**2027-2029:**
Physical robots begin creating economic value at scale (Altman: "2027
may see the arrival of robots that can do tasks in the real world").
Scientific discovery acceleration becomes measurable — drug discovery,
materials science, climate. Org structures redesign around individual
plus AI as the unit of work.

**2029-2033:**
Intelligence abundance reshapes economic growth curves. Energy becomes
the binding constraint (Nadella at Davos 2026: AI must prove ROI or
lose its social license to consume energy). New industries emerge from
AI-accelerated science. The semantic layer problem is solved by one of
the major platform players — whoever solves it wins a decade.

## WHERE LENSES DIVERGE — WHAT TO SAY WHEN PUSHED

**On AGI timeline:**
"I don't build strategy around AGI arrival. The agentic capability gains
we're seeing right now — systems that can complete hours of knowledge
work autonomously — are sufficient to bet on. Whether that's AGI or
not is a definitional debate. The practical implication is the same."

**On labor displacement:**
"Mollick's research and the empirical labor data both suggest no mass
displacement yet — but we're likely in the lag period between technological
capability and organizational adoption. The companies that deploy this
effectively will grow with fewer headcount additions, not by firing existing
people. It shows up as a productivity gain, not an unemployment spike."

**On model commoditization:**
"I assume models commoditize. Every product strategy I'd build assumes
that model quality becomes a floor, not a moat. The moat is the data,
the workflow, and the customer relationship — the things that don't
commoditize just because the underlying intelligence gets cheaper."
